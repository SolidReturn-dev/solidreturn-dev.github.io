What are virtual influencers?

Virtual influencers are computer-generated characters or avatars created through advanced technologies, such as computer graphics, artificial intelligence (AI), and 3D modeling. Unlike traditional influencers who are real people, virtual influencers are entirely digital and may have their own personalities, lifestyles, and unique traits.

These digital entities are often designed to engage with audiences on social media platforms, much like human influencers. They can share content, endorse products, and interact with followers. The goal is to create a lifelike and relatable presence that resonates with online communities.

Virtual influencers are a product of the intersection between technology and influencer marketing, offering a novel way for brands to connect with audiences. While some appreciate the creativity and innovation behind virtual influencers, others raise ethical questions about the potential impact on authenticity and transparency in online interactions.

While the concept of virtual influencers isn't entirely novel—dating back to the introduction of virtual Japanese popstar Kyoko Date in 1996—recent technological advancements have propelled them into the limelight.

Termed digital influencers or AI influencers, these computer-generated personalities maintain an active presence on social media, engaging with the world through a first-person perspective. Crafted by skilled 3D artists utilizing CGI (computer-generated imagery), motion-capture technology, and AI tools, these entities offer creators the freedom to tailor appearances and behaviors to precise specifications. Their personas are meticulously curated to resonate with a target audience.

Virtual influencers fall into three primary categories: non-humans, animated humans, and lifelike CGI humans. Each category presents an innovative avenue for connecting with audiences in ways previously unexplored.

Why do virtual influencers exist?

Virtual influencers exist for several reasons, primarily driven by the evolving landscape of technology, marketing, and entertainment. Here are some key reasons for the existence of virtual influencers:

  1. Innovative Marketing: Virtual influencers represent a cutting-edge approach to marketing. Brands are constantly seeking novel ways to capture audience attention and virtual influencers provide a unique, technologically-driven avenue for promotion.
  2. Customization and Control: Brands and creators have complete control over the appearance, personality, and behavior of virtual influencers. This level of customization allows for the creation of characters that perfectly align with a brand's image or messaging strategy.
  3. 24/7 Availability: Unlike human influencers who require downtime, virtual influencers can be active 24/7, consistently engaging with audiences across different time zones and maintaining a continuous online presence.
  4. Cost-Effectiveness: While the initial investment in creating a virtual influencer can be significant, they may be more cost-effective in the long run. Virtual influencers don't require traditional influencer fees, contracts, or the logistics associated with human influencers.
  5. Global Appeal: Virtual influencers, being digital entities, can transcend cultural and language barriers more easily. This global appeal makes them attractive to brands with international audiences.
  6. Creative Content: Virtual influencers provide an opportunity for creative storytelling and content creation. They can participate in scenarios and narratives that might be challenging or impractical for real individuals.
  7. Targeted Audiences: Creators can design virtual influencers with specific demographics and interests in mind, ensuring that their content resonates with a particular target audience.
  8. Technological Advancements: Advances in CGI, motion-capture technology, and artificial intelligence have made it easier and more realistic to create virtual influencers. The increasing accessibility of these technologies has contributed to the rise of virtual influencers

In essence, virtual influencers offer a new and innovative approach to influencer marketing, leveraging technology to create engaging and customizable brand ambassadors. Their existence reflects the dynamic intersection of entertainment, technology, and marketing strategies in the digital age.

Progress in artificial intelligence, the surge of social media, and the envisaging of the metaverse—where real and virtual realms seamlessly intertwine to form an expansive immersive digital experience—have collaboratively propelled the ascent of virtual influencers.

The surge in their popularity has motivated marketing agencies to wholeheartedly adopt them as an economically efficient promotional strategy.

While real influencers, boasting millions of followers, may command hefty sums, potentially reaching hundreds of thousands of dollars per post, a 2020 estimate suggested that the virtual influencer Lil Miquela took a more modest approach, charging a seemingly reasonable £6,550 (equivalent to about A$12,600 at present).

The advantages of virtual influencers in the realm of online engagement and marketing are evident. They defy the constraints of aging, remain untarnished by real-world scandals, and possess the unique ability to articulate any language they are programmed for. Consequently, it comes as no surprise that numerous companies and celebrities have enthusiastically embraced this trend.

In 2019, the supermodel Bella Hadid made waves by posing alongside Lil Miquela in advertisements for Calvin Klein, an event that one columnist described as a "terrifying glimpse of the future."

Since then, virtual influencers have only soared in popularity. In 2021, Prada notably introduced a CGI ambassador for its perfume, "Candy." More recently, Lil Miquela has graced high-profile brand campaigns and celebrity interviews, solidifying her presence in the mainstream. Even rapper Timbaland has expressed contemplation about a potential collaboration in this intriguing realm.

Pioneering Profit in the Age of Avatars:

The advent of virtual replicas, including the controversial deepfakes, has ignited new debates surrounding the ethical use of a person's likeness, whether granted willingly or not.

On one front, there's a concerning surge in celebrity deepfake adult content. Simultaneously, celebrities are proactively incorporating "simulation rights" into their contracts, granting permission for their digital likeness to be utilized in various contexts. A notable example is soccer legend Lionel Messi, who sanctioned PepsiCo to employ a digital iteration of himself in the promotion of Lay's potato chips.

While this trend opens avenues for broadening the scope of talent utilization, it concurrently amplifies the specter of exploitation. Individuals may find themselves inadvertently or desperately relinquishing control over their digital likeness, either without proper consent or fair compensation. This dynamic intersection between technological innovation and ethical considerations underscores the evolving challenges in navigating the lucrative yet precarious terrain of digital avatars.

The Ongoing Saga: Virtual vs. Human Influencers

The prospect of the virtual supplanting the human remains a tantalizing question. Currently, the dynamics suggest a more harmonious coexistence than an outright substitution. Virtual influencers, at least for now, fall short in establishing the genuine connections that real individuals effortlessly forge, though the future trajectory of this capability remains uncertain.

For human content creators, the presence of virtual influencers serves as both a wellspring of inspiration and a formidable competitor. This digital wave is redefining the essence of creativity and influence in the online realm. Whether embraced willingly or not, human creators find themselves compelled to navigate this evolving landscape, working with—or at the very least, alongside—virtual influencers in whatever collaborative capacities may emerge. The intersection of the human and the virtual heralds a transformative era, where cooperation and adaptation become the keys to navigating the dynamic world of online influence.
